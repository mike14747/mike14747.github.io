Sauerkraut Recipe

This is my favorite sauerkraut recipe. It doesn't use any salt and it's just raw, fermented veggies... nothing else added.

Some people think you need to use salt as a brining agent when making sauerkraut. Not true! Cabbage naturally contains all the pre/pro biotics neccessary for the process.

The two key things I do to unleash those key pre/pro biotics are:

  1. First, blend the cores of the cabbage with enough water to make a slurry in a high-speed blender. Then, mix this slurry with the rest of the ingredients.
  2. The second thing I do to accelerate the brining process is to thoroughly and vigorously massage the shredded cabbage.

Now, onto my raw, no-salt sauerkraut recipe (note: I've included a standard size table spoon in many of the pics as a reference in lieu of explicit portion sizes)...

Give it a little kick

Coarsely chop turmeric (upper left), ginger root (lower left), dried chili peppers (lower right), garlic (upper right).

You can use any or all of these items. I prefer all!

Orange looks good on/in you

Grate a carrot or two (depending on their size).

This will add a little color to a normal bland looking item.

Add a little texture

Coarsely chop an onion (upper left), fennel (lower middle) and thinly slice daikon half moons (upper right).

These items ferment very well, while still retaining a bit of a crunch.

Get your iodine

Wakame (sea vegetable)... coarsely cut with scissors and rehydrated before adding to the mix.

Aside from adding color and flovor to the sauerkraut, Sea vegetables are an excellent source of iodine.

Start the brining

Cabbage cores blended in high speed blender (in this case, a Vitamix) with water added to make it a puree.

This will be step one in jumpstarting the brining process.

Keep on brining

Coarsely chop a couple heads of green cabbage. Vigorously massage it to help break down the fibers in advance. Mix it up with some nappa or red cabbage for variety if youâ€™d like.

This step accelerates the brining process.

Finishing it off

Mix all ingredients. Place whole cabbage leaves over the top of the mixture to prevent oxidation, with a plate going over them and a weight on top of that (eg: a gallon of water). Place a towel over the container to keep bugs out. This will yield several quarts.

The end is in sight

The liquid level should start at about an inch above the bottom and slowly rise. By the second day, the mixture should be fully or nearly fully submerged.

After 2.5 to 4 days (depending upon the ambient temperature and the level of fermentation you prefer), the sauerkraut is done!
